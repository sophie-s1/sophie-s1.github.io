1. Introduction to Polymer Science

A polymer ("many parts") is a macromolecule consisting of many monomer units covalently bonded together to form a long chain.

Nomenclature and Classification

  • Naming: Use the prefix "poly" followed by the monomer name (e.g., polyethylene). Brackets are required for complex names like poly(vinyl chloride).
  • Oligomers: Molecules with a molecular weight () g/mol.
  • Polymers: Molecules with g/mol.
  • Types: Polymers can be organic, inorganic, natural (like DNA), or synthetic (like PVC or Polystyrene) .

Polymer Architectures

Polymers can be synthesized in various 3D shapes at the molecular level:

  • Linear
  • Ring, Star, Comb, or Ladder.
  • Dendrimers: Highly branched, well-defined structures with a central core, repetitive branching units, and a terminal surface.

Shape and Conformation

Polymers are not linear; they adopt a loosely coiled ball shape due to bond angles and rotational freedom.

  • Bond Angles: C-C bonds (0.154 nm) exist at an angle of 109°.
  • Rotational Conformations: Chains rotate around bonds into stable (trans: 180°, gauche: 60° or 300°) or unstable (cis: 0°) states.
  • Shapes: A chain of 20,000 carbons has possible shapes.

4. Isomerism in Polymers

There are three main types of isomerism that dictate polymer properties:

  1. Stereoisomerism (Tacticity): Concerns the arrangement of chiral centres within the backbone.

    • Atactic: Random arrangement; usually amorphous.
    • Isotactic: All substituents on the same side; semi-crystalline helix.
    • Syndiotactic: Alternating sides; semi-crystalline zig-zag or helix.

  2. Regioisomerism: Concerns how monomers join, typically head-to-tail. Head-to-head joins are infrequent.

  3. Geometrical Isomerism: Occurs in diene polymers (e.g., isoprene) as cis or trans configurations around a double bond.

    • Example: Cis-polyisoprene is natural rubber (elastomer), while trans-polyisoprene is gutta-percha (rigid).

5. Physical and Thermal Properties

Crystallinity

Most commercial polymers are semi-crystalline, containing both ordered (crystallites) and disordered (amorphous) regions. A polymer can never be 100% crystalline. Higher crystallinity generally increases strength.

Thermal Transitions

  • Melting Temperature (): The temperature where crystalline domains lose structure and melt. It is a sharp transition.

6. Glass Transition Temperature ()

The Glass Transition Temperature () is a property associated exclusively with the amorphous regions of a polymer. It marks the transition from a hard, brittle "glassy" state to a soft, flexible "rubbery" state due to rotation in the backbone.

Molecular Mechanism

  • Backbone Rotation: represents the onset of backbone rotation, where individual segments of the polymer chain gain enough thermal energy to rotate freely.
  • Below : Chains are "frozen" in place, resulting in a glassy material.
  • Above : Chains can rotate, allowing for rubbery behaviour.

Structural Factors Affecting

Any aspect of the polymer structure that hinders backbone rotation will increase :

  1. Backbone Stiffness: Rigid linkages (like aromatic rings) increase significantly.
  2. Substituent Polarity: Stronger intermolecular forces (e.g., hydrogen bonding in poly(vinyl alcohol)) increase .
  3. Steric Hindrance: Large, bulky side groups (like the benzene ring in polystyrene) restrict rotation and raise .
  4. Side Chain Flexibility: Longer, flexible side chains (e.g., in poly(alkyl methacrylates)) can actually decrease by increasing "free volume".
  5. Tacticity: The spatial arrangement of substituents has a small but definite effect on .

8. Polymer Morphology

Polymers are semi-crystalline, meaning they consist of ordered crystalline regions (crystallites) embedded in a disordered amorphous matrix.

Hierarchy of Structure

  1. Unit Cell: The smallest repeating unit of the crystal19. Polymer unit cells are often large and complex due to chain restrictions.
  2. Lamellae: Crystalline "sheets" formed by chains folding back and forth. In reality, chains often leave one lamella to entangle in amorphous regions before entering another.
  3. Spherulites: Large, spherical structures visible under a microscope, composed of many lamellae radiating from a central nucleus.

10. Kinetics of Crystallization

Crystallization occurs in two stages: nucleation (forming a stable "seed") and growth (adding chains to the nucleus).

Types of Nucleation

  • Homogeneous (Sporadic): Nuclei form randomly.
  • Heterogeneous: Growth is "seeded" by foreign particles (dust, nucleating agents). This allows for the control of spherulite size; more nuclei lead to smaller spherulites, which improves mechanical properties.

12. Chain Polymerisation

Chain polymerisation proceeds via the sequential addition of monomers to an active centre (free-radical, anion, or cation).

The Three Stages

  1. Initiation: Creation of active centres using chemical initiators (e.g., AIBN, BPO), UV, or radiation. Initiator efficiency () accounts for radicals lost to side reactions like the "cage effect".

  2. Propagation: Rapid growth as monomers add to the active chain end.

  3. Termination: Growth stops when two active centres react.

    • Combination: Two chains join (common for styrene); .
    • Disproportionation: Hydrogen atom transfer creates one saturated and one unsaturated chain (common for methacrylates); .

Kinetics and Characteristics

  • High molecular weight chains are achieved early in the reaction.
  • Increasing initiator concentration increases the reaction rate but decreases the resulting molecular weight.
  • Suspectable to chain transfer, forming side products.
  • unwanted long side chains can be formed when a chain bonds in the middle of another chain's backbone.

Inhibition and retardation:

Retarders react with radicals to form a product that is incapable of further growth.
Highly effective retarders are called inhibitors and can be added to monomers to prevent spontaneous polymerisation in long-term storage then remove via distillation.

13. Different Polymerisation Techniques

Anionic and Cationic Polymerisation

  • Anionic: Involves anionic active ends that cannot react with each other, leading to "living" polymerisation with no inherent termination. This allows for excellent control over molecular mass (Ð).
  • Cationic: Extremely fast but suffers from high rates of termination and chain transfer, offering poor control over molecular weight.

16. Controlled and Controlled-Radical Polymerization

Controlled polymerizations are specialized chain reactions designed to produce polymers with highly predictable molecular weights and narrow molecular weight distributions (low dispersity).

Key Characteristics

  • Low Dispersity: Typically (often referred to as monodisperse).
  • Linear Growth: The molecular weight increases linearly with monomer conversion.
  • Living Character: Active chain ends remain functional after 100% conversion, allowing for "one-pot" block copolymer synthesis through sequential monomer addition.
  • Suppressed Termination: In Controlled-Radical Polymerization (CRP), termination is suppressed (though not eliminated) relative to the rate of propagation. In "pure" Controlled Polymerization (CP), termination is effectively eliminated.

18. Group Transfer (GTP) & Ring Opening (ROP)

Group Transfer Polymerization (GTP)

  • Concept: A trialkylsilyl group is transferred along the chain during propagation.
  • Application: Primarily used for acrylates and methacrylates.
  • Pros/Cons: Less stringent conditions than anionic (no high vacuum needed), but difficult to achieve very high molecular weights ( g/mol).

Ring-Opening Polymerization (ROP)

  • Concept: Cyclic monomers (lactones, lactams, cyclic ethers) are "opened" to relieve ring strain.
  • Mechanism: Can be anionic, cationic, or radical depending on the initiator.
  • Utility: Excellent for producing biodegradable polymers like polyesters.

19. Atom Transfer Radical Polymerization (ATRP)

ATRP is a versatile CRP technique that uses a transition metal catalyst to control radical growth.

Mechanism

  • Dormant vs. Active: Chains spend most of their time in a "dormant" halide-capped state ().
  • Equilibrium: A Copper(I) catalyst/ligand complex reversibly abstracts the halogen to create a "reactive" polymer radical () for propagation.
  • Components: Requires an alkyl halide initiator, a monomer (styrenics, methacrylates, acrylates), and a transition metal catalyst (typically Cu, Fe, or Ru) with solubilizing ligands.

20. Reversible Addition-Fragmentation Chain Transfer (RAFT)

RAFT is a CRP technique that relies on a dithioester-based Chain Transfer Agent (CTA) to maintain living character.

Key Features

  • Mechanism: Rapid, reversible transfer of a group between dormant and active chains.
  • Control: The target depends on the concentration of the CTA, not the initiator ().
  • Versatility: Works under bulk, organic, and aqueous conditions and is tolerant of many functional groups (including acids).

Gel Permeation Chromatography (GPC)

GPC, also known as Size Exclusion Chromatography (SEC), separates polymer chains based on their size in solution.

  • Process: A polymer solution is pumped through columns packed with porous beads.
  • Mechanism: Smaller molecules enter the pores and take longer to elute; larger molecules bypass the pores and elute first.
  • Detectors: Generally uses Refractive Index (RI), Viscosity, or UV detectors.

NMR Spectroscopy in Polymer Science

Used for several critical calculations:

  1. Calculation: Comparing peak areas of initiator/end-protons to backbone protons.
  2. Polymerization Kinetics: Following the disappearance of monomer vinyl peaks over time to calculate % conversion.
  3. Copolymer Composition: Determining the molar and volume fractions of different blocks in a copolymer.
